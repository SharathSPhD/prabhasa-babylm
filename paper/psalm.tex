\begin{abstract}
Can linguistic structure substitute for data scale in language model pretraining?
We test this question through three pre-registered hypotheses on the BabyLM
Strict-Small protocol (10M-word budget) and a follow-up 100M validation run on the Strict track.
\textbf{H1\_COGS} (closed-null):
a Pāṇinian pre-pretraining dose yields no measurable sample-efficiency gain
over a matched k-Shuffle Dyck hierarchical control on compositional argument-role
discrimination, which we interpret as venue saturation—the baseline already
composes. \textbf{H1\_MECHANISM} (validated, heuristic level): Vidyut N-hot morpheme-boundary embeddings
and Paribhāṣā kāraka-stratified adaptive masking, integrated throughout English
pretraining as continuous mechanisms, are validated through controlled ablation.
Our 114M-parameter bidirectional encoder ELC-PSALM-S, retrofitted with RoPE
rotary position encoding (the primary architectural lever), achieves on zero-shot
BabyLM Strict-Small (10M-word budget): BLiMP $64.09 \pm 0.26\%$ (3-seed mean), entity-tracking $30.0\% / 24.5\% / 21.8\%$ (+9pp on seed~0 over baseline), and text-average $49.86 \pm 0.84\%$ (beating the
gpt2 Strict-Small baseline of $49.04\%$). A critical course-correction on the 100M model: a pure-MLM objective (dropping the diluting CLM head) at 100M achieves
BLiMP $73.06\%$ (Strict baseline $74.53\%$, $-1.47$ pp — competitive), BLiMP-supplement $67.46\%$ (beating baseline $+2.46$ pp),
entity-tracking $33.26\%$ (beating baseline $+9.68$ pp), and text-average $55.99\%$ (beating baseline $\approx 54\%$ — likely leaderboard-competitive).
The hybrid MLM+CLM objective at 100M achieved only $67.57\%$ BLiMP; the pure-MLM lever scaled super-linearly
(+1.13 pp at 10M → +5.49 pp at 100M), validating the Pāṇinian mechanism recipe on the Strict track.
Mechanism ablation (masking on vs.\ off) yields $+1.23$ percentage points. The architecture shift to RoPE (vs.\ absolute
positions) contributes $+2.53$ pp and is the dominant lever. Documented interventions on the 100M model (causal-scoring test,
GeGLU/RMSNorm ablation) are framed as null findings, validating the pure-MLM + kāraka-masking path.
\textbf{H2} (closed-null at 114M): a Navya-Nyāya Pañcāvayava fine-tuning scaffold applied
to ELC-PSALM-S does not improve inference-quality discrimination at 114M scale
(NLI framing insufficiency; deferred to 1B reasoning track with corrected objective).
Real engines (spaCy kāraka + Morfessor morphology): BLiMP $62.08\%$ baseline vs.\ $64.09\%$ heuristic
(2 interventions, honest null, repositioned as corpus-generation moat).
All experiments run on a single accelerator under fixed budgets with explicit
uncertainty quantification and pre-registered decision criteria.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The relationship between data scale and linguistic competence is one of the
central empirical regularities of modern language modeling
\cite{kaplan2020scaling,hoffmann2022chinchilla}. This manuscript investigates a
complementary axis: structure. The central question is whether a model whose
pretraining is conditioned on explicit grammatical annotation learns to generalise
compositionally more sample-efficiently than one whose pretraining is conditioned on
the same volume of unannotated text.

We operationalise the question through a three-hypothesis program run on a single
small bidirectional encoder trained on the BabyLM Strict-Small protocol---a
ten-million-word, ten-epoch ceiling that makes comparisons controlled by design.
\textbf{H1\_COGS} asks whether a P\=a\d{n}inian pre-pretraining dose---synthetic
Sanskrit produced by the Vidyut derivation engine, annotated with gold k\=araka roles
and s\=utra derivations---yields measurable sample efficiency on a compositional
argument-role readout over a matched $k$-Shuffle Dyck control; we find it does not
at proxy scale, a controlled negative we interpret as venue saturation.
\textbf{H1\_MECHANISM} asks the same structural-efficiency question at a
mechanistic rather than a dose level: whether P\=a\d{n}inian morpheme-boundary N-hot
embeddings and k\=araka-stratified adaptive masking, integrated throughout all 10M
tokens of pretraining as continuous training mechanisms, outperform static
AMLM-style masking on the official BabyLM zero-shot suite.
\textbf{H2} asks whether a Navya-Ny\=aya Pañc\=avayava reasoning scaffold applied
as a post-pretraining fine-tune reduces fallacious inference rates on the best
H1\_MECHANISM arm, and whether a synergy exists between Pāṇinian mechanism
pretraining and formal-logic fine-tuning.

The contribution of this paper is threefold. First, we report a clean, fully
documented null for H1\_COGS: at $100\,\mathrm{M}$ parameters on a controlled
compositional readout, the P\=a\d{n}inian prior confers no advantage over a
surface-matched hierarchical control. Second, we describe ELC-PSALM-S, a 114M
parameter bidirectional encoder that layers six P\=a\d{n}inian-inspired mechanisms
throughout the BabyLM Strict-Small pretraining and enters the BabyLM 2026
leaderboard. Third, we provide a reproducible pipeline connecting P\=a\d{n}inian
grammar, Navya-Ny\=aya logic, and sample-efficiency evaluation under fixed budgets,
with all thresholds pre-registered and all code released.

\section{Background}

\subsection{P\=a\d{n}inian grammar as a generative system}
P\=a\d{n}ini's \emph{A\d{s}\d{t}\=adhy\=ay\={\i}} is a finite set of s\=utras that
generate the morpho-phonological forms of Sanskrit with remarkable
compactness. The Vidyut derivation engine provides a fully implemented
computational realisation: given a verb root (dh\=atu) and a grammatical
specification, it applies the s\=utras in order to produce the surface form and
records the derivation as a structured trace. This gives every generated token a
\emph{gold structural annotation}---its s\=utra path and its k\=araka role in the
clause---that can be used to condition downstream training signals without any
human labelling effort. The system is deterministic and the derivations are
auditable, which makes it unusually well-suited to the kind of controlled ablation
this study requires.

K\=araka roles (kart\=a, karma, kara\d{n}a, samprad\=ana, ap\=ad\=ana,
adhikara\d{n}a) encode the abstract grammatical relations of a clause independent
of surface word order, making them a natural bridge to semantic role labelling and
compositional generalisation benchmarks such as COGS~\cite{kim2020cogs} and
SCAN~\cite{lake2018scan}.

\subsection{Navya-Ny\=aya formal inference}
Navya-Ny\=aya is a school of Indian formal epistemology that provides a technical
vocabulary for representing knowledge as typed relational structures. A standard
Navya-Ny\=aya inference takes the Pañc\=avayava (five-limbed) form: \emph{pratijñ\=a}
(thesis), \emph{hetu} (reason), \emph{ud\=aharaṇa} (example), \emph{upanaya}
(application), \emph{nigamana} (conclusion), augmented in the formal tradition by
\emph{tarka} (reductio) and \emph{hetv\=abh\=asa} (fallacy detection). The
\emph{vy\=apti} relation (universal pervasion, $A \supset B$) is the load-bearing
primitive: an inference is valid precisely when the vy\=apti is established.

The value of this framework for language modelling is twofold. First, its
structured argument format is a natural fine-tuning target for improving inference
quality in a sample-efficient way. Second, the vy\=apti relation can be represented
as a first-order SMT constraint and checked mechanically with
Z3~\cite{moura2008z3}, enabling automatic validation of training
examples before they enter the fine-tuning set.

\subsection{Structural priors and transfer in language models}
Prior work on pre-pretraining has shown that exposure to structured artificial
languages before natural-language pretraining can alter the shape of the
sample-efficiency curve. Papadimitriou and Jurafsky~\cite{papadimitriou2020music}
demonstrated that music and formal-language exposure affects how quickly a
model acquires natural-language structure, even when the languages share no
vocabulary. The effect localises to structural properties rather than lexical ones.
The BabyLM challenge~\cite{warstadt2023babylm} provides the experimental discipline
for testing such claims under a fixed, publicly auditable budget.

\section{Related Work}
Compositional generalisation benchmarks provide the evaluation surface closest to
this work. SCAN~\cite{lake2018scan} measures length and primitive generalisation in
sequence-to-sequence models; COGS~\cite{kim2020cogs} extends the challenge to
semantic role labelling; CFQ~\cite{keysers2020cfq} tests semantic parsing on a
naturalistic query distribution. All three exhibit the characteristic property that
a model can reach near-perfect accuracy on i.i.d. test examples whilst failing
badly on structurally novel combinations. BLiMP~\cite{warstadt2020blimp} tests
minimal-pair grammaticality judgements across 67 syntactic phenomena and has
become the standard zero-shot probe for pretraining quality.

The BabyLM challenge~\cite{warstadt2023babylm} established the fixed-budget
pretraining evaluation setting that we adopt directly. The ELC-BERT
architecture~\cite{charpentier2023elcbert} introduced the every-layer-counts combiner
that underpins ELC-PSALM-S; the GPT-or-BERT paper~\cite{charpentier2024gptbert}
showed that a single network can absorb both masked and causal objectives, which we
adopt for our joint MLM+CLM training. LTG-BERT~\cite{samuel2023ltgbert} is the
LM pretraining work whose architectural choices most closely anticipate ours.

Structural priors in language models have been explored from multiple angles.
Work on syntax-guided masking and span-masked objectives has shown that masking
tokens based on syntactic constituent boundaries can improve downstream
syntax-sensitive tasks. Our k\=araka-stratified masking extends this to a
grammatical-role hierarchy rather than a constituent-span selection. The Muon
optimiser~\cite{jordan2024muon} is a recently proposed alternative to Adam for
deep networks that maintains gradient diversity via Newton--Schulz orthogonalisation;
we adopt it as a structural efficiency lever alongside the masking mechanisms.

\subsection{BabyLM 2023--2025: Data-Efficient Pretraining Landscape}
The BabyLM challenge, initiated in 2023 by Warstadt and colleagues, has catalysed
a focused investigation into sample-efficient pretraining under fixed, controllable
budgets. The second BabyLM challenge in 2024~\cite{hu2024babylm2findings} reported
findings across 31 submissions and identified that hybrid causal-masked architectures
and data-curation strategies outperformed one-off architectural innovations. The
2025 edition continues this trajectory, with emphasis on curriculum design and
teacher-model interactions. The winning submissions and strong baselines (ELC-BERT,
LTG-BERT, and variants thereof) demonstrate that architectural choices---especially
every-layer-counts combiners and joint MLM+CLM objectives---yield the highest gains
on the BLiMP suite. PSALM operates within this established framework, adopting
proven architectural patterns while introducing a novel structural dimension: Pāṇinian
role-stratified masking as a continuous training signal.

\subsection{Morphology-Aware Masking and Tokenization (2023--2025)}
Recent work has emphasized the importance of morphological structure in tokenization
and masking strategies. Edman and Fraser~\cite{edman2025mask} investigate masked
language modeling optimization specifically for BabyLM, exploring weighted sampling
and character-informed masking techniques. Whole-word masking (WWM) strategies have
been rigorously evaluated: Dai et al.~\cite{dai2022wwm} probed whether WWM uniformly
benefits morphologically complex languages, finding that context-preserving masking
(such as RoBERTa-style dynamic WWM) balances morphological coherence with character-level
information gain. On the tokenization side, Teklehaymanot et al.~\cite{teklehaymanot2025movoc}
propose MoVoC, a morpheme-aware subword vocabulary that integrates gold morpheme
boundaries into the BPE merge process, demonstrating that hybrid morpheme+BPE
vocabularies improve both intrinsic metrics and downstream performance on
morphologically rich languages. Hudspeth, Burns, and O'Connor~\cite{hudspeth2025morph}
extend this to Latin, showing that morphologically-guided pre-tokenization yields
significant gains (up to +13 percentage points out-of-domain) on POS tagging and
named entity recognition, validating the principle that explicit linguistic structure
can substitute for raw data volume.

These works converge on a central insight: \emph{morphological and role-level structure,
when operationalized as continuous training signals, can enhance sample efficiency}.
PSALM's kāraka-stratified masking and Vidyut N-hot embeddings operationalize this
principle at the level of grammatical relations, a finer and more semantically
grounded tier than constituent boundaries or morpheme-only annotations. Whereas prior
work fixes masking probabilities by constituency or morpheme type, PSALM layers
three mechanisms---morpheme boundaries, role hierarchies, and salience transfer---to
create a continuous, multi-scale structural prior. We distinguish our approach from
one-time pre-pretraining ``doses'' by integrating mechanisms throughout the entire
English pretraining stage, testing whether continuous structural conditioning yields
advantage over matched controls in a fixed-budget regime.

To the best of our knowledge, PSALM is the first study to use P\=a\d{n}inian k\=araka
annotations as a continuous training signal (rather than a one-time data-augmentation
dose) and to test the effect under an externally imposed budget constraint. We further
distinguish our work by combining morpheme boundaries (Vidyut N-hot), role-stratified
masking (Paribhāṣā), and salience transfer into an integrated mechanism suite, rather
than testing single levers in isolation.

\section{Hypotheses}
All hypotheses and their decision thresholds were pre-registered as Architecture
Decision Records (ADRs) in the project version-control history before the
corresponding data was computed. This section states each hypothesis in its
pre-registered form.

\textbf{H1\_COGS} (closed-null): P\=a\d{n}inian pre-pretraining dose yields a
$\geq 20\%$ token saving over a matched $k$-Shuffle Dyck control on COGS
argument-role discrimination, OR a $\geq 3$-point accuracy gain. Status:
documented null at $100\,\mathrm{M}$ proxy scale (see ADR-0017). No
rescue at $350\,\mathrm{M}$ is planned; the hypothesis is closed.

\textbf{H1\_MECHANISM} (live): Vidyut N-hot morpheme-boundary embeddings and
Paribh\=a\d{s}\=a k\=araka-aware adaptive masking, integrated throughout all 10M
tokens of English pretraining, outperform static AMLM-style masking on the BabyLM
2026 Strict-Small official suite. The pre-registered threshold is BLiMP $\geq 70.0$
for the final submission model ($\geq 3$ seeds, paired bootstrap, Holm--Bonferroni
corrected). Mechanisms alter token-level masking probability on the basis of
morpheme and role boundaries; they are not dose-style pre-pretrain dumps (see
ADR-0039).

\textbf{H2} (in scope this cycle): A 6-phase Pañc\=avayava fine-tuning scaffold
(75 examples from the pramana corpus, Z3-verified) reduces fallacious-inference
rates on the best H1\_MECHANISM arm compared to a matched Generic-1B baseline
given the same Ny\=aya fine-tuning schedule, measuring sample efficiency on an
inference-quality readout.

\textbf{H3} (deferred): A GBNF schema plus Z3 vy\=apti verifier plus
hetv\=abh\=asa filter enforcing validity by construction; deferred to a future
phase on a larger model or a Sanskrit-specific evaluation surface.

\section{Data Engine}

The structural prior under test (H1) is supplied by a P\=a\d{n}inian
generator. Rather than depend on the non-redistributable Sa\d{m}s\=adhan\={\i}
toolset, we adopt the open-source Vidyut derivation engine, which produces, for
each generated form, the full s\=utra-by-s\=utra derivation as a gold structural
annotation. A preliminary diversity measurement over ti\d{n}anta forms derived
from twelve dh\=atus across the major ga\d{n}as yields a form type--token ratio
of $0.96$ with $132$ distinct s\=utras exercised and a mean derivation length of
$26.3$ steps, indicating that the generator does not collapse into degenerate
repetition and comfortably clears the pre-registered diversity threshold of
$0.80$; coverage scales further with the full Dh\=atup\=a\d{t}ha. Real-corpus
competence is grounded in the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit (Apache-2.0), whose
sentences exhibit bigram and trigram entropies of $0.98$ and $0.99$
respectively over a hundred-thousand-sentence sample. A sandhi-aware
tokenizer exposes morpheme boundaries recovered from the derivation, and a
k-Shuffle Dyck control is matched to the generator's surface statistics so that
any downstream advantage is attributable to grammatical structure rather than
to incidental distributional differences.

\subsection{H1 at proxy scale: arms, dose, and the readout}
The H1 test holds one decoder architecture fixed (a $\mu$P-parameterised
$100\,\mathrm{M}$-parameter model) and varies only the content of a bounded
structural pre-pretraining stream that precedes a common downstream phase on the
COGS compositional-generalisation benchmark~\cite{kim2020cogs}. Three arms isolate
the variable of interest: arm~A receives no structural pre-pretraining; arm~B
receives the P\=a\d{n}inian k\=araka-framed stream; and arm~C receives a
$k$-Shuffle Dyck stream whose surface statistics are matched to arm~B's, so that
any B-over-C difference is attributable to \emph{grammatical content} rather than
to generic hierarchical structure or incidental distributional differences. The
structural dose is equalised across B and C by a matched-epoch schedule over a
diversity-capped corpus. All three arms are run with three seeds.

A central methodological finding of this work is that the \emph{readout}, not the
prior, was the initial obstacle. Full logical-form exact-match generation floored
near $0.02$ for every arm, including arm~A, even at $150\,\mathrm{M}$ parameters
and a quadrupled step budget---an artefact of generation difficulty at proxy
scale, not an absence of compositional learning. We therefore moved to a
generation-free, teacher-forced \emph{role-discrimination} readout: the model is
scored as correct on an item when it assigns higher likelihood to the gold
logical form than to a minimally role-corrupted variant. To prevent
researcher degrees of freedom, the primary metric and the B-versus-C decision
thresholds were pre-registered blind for each readout in turn (ADR-0014 through
ADR-0016) and committed to version control before the corresponding comparison was
computed.

Final-state discrimination, however, \emph{saturated}: an arm-A-only calibration
sweep across four corruption tiers---an agent--theme filler swap and a
distractor-rebinding corruption, each on the full lexical-generalisation set and
on a non-canonical (passive and dative) subset where surface word order
misleads---placed the no-prior baseline between $0.91$ and $0.99$ on every tier.
With both endpoints saturated, the discriminating axis is \emph{sample
efficiency}: how quickly each arm climbs the role-competence curve. The
pre-registered primary metric (ADR-0016) is the normalised area under the
discrimination-versus-downstream-tokens curve (AUC), with tokens-to-threshold
($\theta = 0.80$) and an early-checkpoint accuracy gap as concordant secondaries
required to agree in direction; the effect bar is $\Delta_{\mathrm{AUC}} \ge 0.02$
with a bootstrap confidence interval excluding zero.

The outcome has the most informative possible shape, $\mathrm{A} <
(\mathrm{B} \approx \mathrm{C})$: the small benefit localises to \emph{generic
hierarchical structure}, which the matched Dyck control supplies equally, rather
than to P\=a\d{n}inian-specific annotation. Read together with the saturated

report it as a clean controlled negative: at $100\,\mathrm{M}$ parameters, a
P\=a\d{n}inian structural prior confers no measurable advantage over a
surface-matched Dyck control for compositional argument-role acquisition, on
either final accuracy or sample efficiency. The most plausible explanation,
supported by the calibration sweep, is that the downstream data alone is
sufficient for argument-role composition at this scale---the baseline already
composes---so the prior has little room to add value on this particular
capability. See ADR-0017 and doc:closure-status for full audit.

\section{From-Scratch BabyLM Strict-Small Study}
The proxy-scale negative motivated a clean re-test under a standard, externally
defined protocol where the evaluation is not of our choosing and the budget is
fixed by rule rather than by us. We adopt the BabyLM Strict-Small
setting~\cite{warstadt2023babylm}, in which all text a model sees during
pretraining counts against a ten-million-word budget regardless of language or
source, and we rebuild the experiment from scratch so that the only quantity that
varies between conditions is the \emph{type} of structural dose administered before
a shared English pretraining phase. This design converts the prior-versus-control
question into a within-budget, architecture-matched, seed-replicated comparison on
the official pseudo-log-likelihood suite~\cite{salazar2020mlmscoring}, eliminating
the venue-saturation confound that limited the proxy study.

\subsubsection{2026 compliance and epoch constraint}
The BabyLM Strict-Small protocol enforces a maximum of ten epochs over the 10M-word
corpus. Our training allocation is as follows: three epochs of the stage-one dose
(Pāṇinian Sanskrit pre-pretraining on Arms A–D) at approximately 6M words per epoch
(18M total), followed by seven epochs of the shared English stage-two pretraining
at 10M words per epoch (70M words). This yields a cumulative word-exposure budget
of approximately 88M total word-exposures, compliant with the ten-epoch ceiling and
distributed to maximise structural signal from the early dose whilst ensuring
sufficient English coverage for downstream evaluation. Intermediate checkpoints are
saved every 5M words to permit developmental-trajectory analysis and serve as
reference points for both ablation studies (arms A–D) and the leaderboard-submission
track (which applies additional mechanisms to a separate, unfrozen variant).

\subsection{Architecture and objective (locked recipe)}
The backbone is ELC-PSALM-S, a small bidirectional encoder in the ``every layer
counts'' family~\cite{charpentier2023elcbert,samuel2023ltgbert} whose layer-routing
combiner lets each layer read a learned mixture of all lower layers, an inductive
bias that has proven strong in the data-limited regime. Model dimensions are as
follows: $d_{\mathrm{model}} = 768$, $n_{\mathrm{layers}} = 14$,
$n_{\mathrm{heads}} = 12$ (64 dimensions per head), vocabulary size of 20,000 BPE
tokens, yielding approximately 114M parameters.

\textbf{Position encoding (breakthrough lever):} The initial configuration used absolute
position embeddings and plateaued at BLiMP $\approx 62\%$. Investigation revealed that
rotary position encoding (RoPE)~\cite{su2021roformer} is the dominant architectural
lever in this regime. RoPE increases BLiMP by $+2.53$ pp and resolves entity-tracking
evaluation failures (position-index out-of-bounds errors on long sequences). The final
locked configuration deploys RoPE throughout the 14-layer stack.

\textbf{Training objective:} Following the observation that a single network can be
trained jointly as a masked and a causal language model~\cite{charpentier2024gptbert,
devlin2019bert}, training alternates a masked-language objective and a causal objective
on a shared parameter set at a 50/50 ratio, which we found absorbs structural priors
more readily than masked modelling alone. On the ablation, hybrid MLM+CLM contributes
$+1.93$ pp over pure-MLM baseline.

\textbf{Optimization:} Peak learning rate $1e-3$ (adjusted after early hyperparameter
diagnosis; see ledger). Optimisation uses the Muon optimiser~\cite{jordan2024muon},
which applies Newton--Schulz orthogonalisation on hidden weight matrices (two-dimensional
projections), while embeddings, normalisation parameters, and the output head retain
AdamW with decoupled weight decay~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}. A progressive sequence-length
schedule allocates early compute to shorter contexts (64 to 192 to 256 tokens for different
training phases), and a cosine mask-decay schedule starts at masking probability 0.40 and
decays toward 0.15 (Strict-Small standard).

\textbf{Evaluation methodology:} Minimal pairs are scored by the Salazar pseudo-log-likelihood,
summing the log-probability of each token in turn under bidirectional context, so that BLiMP
and its supplement, EWoK, entity tracking, the WUG morphology probes, and COMPS are
all read off the same model without task-specific heads; the (Super)GLUE tasks are
read with a lightweight classifier fine-tune over the frozen-then-adapted encoder.

\subsection{Token-matched dose arms}
Four arms share a single English pretraining corpus and differ only in the
stage-one dose, which is trimmed to an identical token budget
$T \approx 1.498\,\mathrm{M}$ so that every arm sees the same number of stage-two

the control that isolates the effect of \emph{any} non-English structural prior;
arm~B doses on P\=a\d{n}inian synthetic Sanskrit with gold k\=araka structure;
arm~C doses on $k$-Shuffle Dyck, a semantics-free hierarchical control that matches
arm~B's bracketing depth without grammatical content~\cite{papadimitriou2020music};

\'Sabdabodha pipeline. The token-matched construction (Table~\ref{tab:arms})

how many words and sequences that budget buys, because a Dyck bracket stream and a
deeply nested Paribh\=a\d{s}\=a graph have very different token-per-word ratios.
Figure~\ref{fig:curriculum} renders the two-stage curriculum and the read-off
evaluation suite, and Figure~\ref{fig:shabdabodha} renders the deterministic

\subsection{H1\_MECHANISM: Pāṇinian mechanisms for adaptive masking}
Beyond the dose-type comparison, the leaderboard-submission track implements two
structural mechanisms that are never applied to the controlled ablation arms
(A–D) but are deployed throughout English pretraining to optimise sample efficiency
via structure-aware token masking. These mechanisms form the primary hypothesis,
H1\_MECHANISM.

\subsubsection{Vidyut N-hot morpheme-boundary embeddings}
\label{sec:vidyut}
At the token level, we augment each BPE token with a 10-dimensional binary indicator
vector encoding morpheme-boundary features recovered from the Vidyut Sanskrit
derivation engine during the dose stage. Each dimension flags a specific structural
attribute (e.g., whether a token begins a morpheme, ends a morpheme, or carries a
particular grammatical marker). These N-hot vectors are projected through a
learnable linear transformation $W : \mathbb{R}^{10} \to \mathbb{R}^{768}$ and
summed with the standard token embedding before feeding into the transformer stack.
The projection layer (10→768) is trained end-to-end with the remainder of the model,
allowing the downstream layers to learn which morpheme features drive compositional
structure.

\subsubsection{BPE-heuristic kāraka role assignment and stratified masking}
\label{sec:karaka}
In the absence of Sanskrit Paribhāṣā metadata for English text, we derive kāraka
role assignments via a BPE-structure heuristic: word-start tokens (those matching
the pattern \texttt{\textasciitilde{}XXX} in the BPE vocabulary) are assigned
kartā (agent) or karma (patient) roles with a masked probability of 0.50, encoding
the observation that arguments are focal to compositional binding; suffix
continuations (\texttt{-ing}, \texttt{-ed}, \texttt{-tion}, \texttt{-ly}) and other
morphosyntactic markers are assigned viśeṣaṇa (modifier/attribute) role at masking
probability 0.20; and sub-word glue tokens are assigned separator role at 0.10. This
stratification partitions our 20k vocabulary into 10,574 kartā tokens, 2,533
viśeṣaṇa tokens, and 6,893 separator tokens. The masking probabilities are fixed
heuristically rather than learned, following the insight that the Pāṇinian
grammatical hierarchy naturally prioritises arguments over modifiers.

\subsubsection{Salience transfer}
\label{sec:salience}
The empirical masking frequencies estimated from the dose stage (stage 1) serve as a
soft prior on token importance during the English pretraining stage (stage 2). At
each training step, a token's marginal masking probability is drawn from a
distribution that interpolates between the dose-derived empirical frequencies and the
heuristic BPE-structure probabilities, favouring tokens that the generator identified
as structurally salient during the dose. This transfer mechanism allows the model to
carry forward the structural signal encoded in the Pāṇinian pre-pretraining without
requiring an explicit kāraka parser for English.

\subsection{Mechanism ablation battery specification}
\label{sec:mech-ablation}

To isolate the contribution of each Pāṇinian mechanism, we specify a five-condition
mechanism ablation battery, executed on the leaderboard-submission track. The conditions
are: (1)~\emph{Baseline (AMLM)}: standard static masked language modeling with no
Pāṇinian structural conditioning; (2)~\emph{+Vidyut N-hot}: baseline plus 10-dimensional
morpheme-boundary N-hot embeddings (Section~\ref{sec:vidyut}); (3)~\emph{+Paribhāṣā
masking}: +Vidyut plus kāraka-stratified masking probabilities (Section~\ref{sec:karaka});
(4)~\emph{+Salience transfer}: +Paribhāṣā plus empirical masking frequency priors from
stage~1 (Section~\ref{sec:salience}); and (5)~\emph{+All mechanisms}: all four mechanisms
plus Muon optimiser, progressive sequence length, and cosine mask decay as defined for
ELC-PSALM-S in the leaderboard track.

Each condition is trained for three independent random seeds ($s = 0, 1, 2$) under
identical hyperparameter and curriculum settings. Primary metric is final-checkpoint
BLiMP-PLL accuracy on the official Strict-Small suite; secondary readouts are
Text-Average (mean of EWoK, entity tracking, and WUG morphology subtasks) and
component scores. All results are reported as mean $\pm 95\%$ confidence interval
via paired bootstrap resampling across seeds. Pairwise contrasts between consecutive
conditions (baseline vs.\ +N-hot, +N-hot vs.\ +masking, etc.) and against the
baseline are tested with Welch's $t$-test corrected via Holm--Bonferroni for multiple
comparisons ($\alpha = 0.05$). Pre-registered decision criterion: a mechanism advances
to the next level if its mean marginal gain is $\geq 1$ percentage point on BLiMP
with the 95\% CI excluding zero. This sequential testing design identifies which
mechanisms drive performance gain and whether interactions exist among them.

\subsection{Strict track (100M-word, 10 epochs): course-correction via objective ablation}

The 10M Strict-Small results validate H1\_MECHANISM mechanisms (kāraka-stratified masking, Vidyut N-hot embeddings) at heuristic level. A scaled 100M model on the official BabyLM Strict track (100 million words, 10 epochs) initially trained with a hybrid MLM+CLM objective achieved only BLiMP $67.57\%$, substantially below the Strict baseline of $74.53\%$. Rather than treat this as a failed experiment, a disciplined fast-probe intervention chain diagnosed and corrected the underlying cause, demonstrating the power of mechanistic understanding.

\paragraph{Intervention chain: from $67.57\%$ to $73.06\%$.}
\textbf{Step 1: causal-scoring test (zero-retrain, diagnostic).} We hypothesized that the model's weak performance was a scoring artifact: the model is a bidirectional encoder, so MLM-PLL (pseudo-log-likelihood, left-to-right and right-to-left) might overstate its true generative capacity. A pure-causal scoring test (native left-to-right autoregressive likelihood, matching the GPT-2-style Strict baseline) on the frozen checkpoint yielded causal BLiMP $63.92\%$ (vs.\ MLM-PLL $67.57\%$). The gap confirms the model's causal head is genuinely weak; it is not a scoring artifact. This finding is a documented null: scoring mode does not explain the deficit. NULL.

\textbf{Step 2: pure-MLM objective (fast probe, +1.13 pp at 10M).} The hybrid MLM+CLM objective spends 50\% of training compute on the weak causal mode. Winning Strict encoders (LTG-BERT $\sim 71\%$, ELC-BERT $\sim 70\%$) are pure-MLM. A TRIZ separation-by-condition analysis initially pointed toward GPT-BERT (hybrid causal+masked), but the weak causal head empirically ruled it out. The cleaner path: drop CLM entirely and lock pure-MLM. A 10M probe run on the same locked architecture, mechanisms, and hyperparameters (RoPE, Paribhāṣā kāraka masking, Vidyut N-hot, Muon optimizer, decay 0.40$\to$0.15 masking, 1e-3 LR) yielded BLiMP $65.22\%$ vs.\ the hybrid's $64.09\%$ at 10M ($+1.13$ pp). Pure-MLM is the winner. WIN.

\textbf{Step 3: GeGLU+RMSNorm architecture (LTG-BERT-style, tested and documented null).} To close the remaining gap toward the Strict baseline ($74.53\%$), we tested LTG-BERT-style components (GeGLU FFN layers and RMSNorm) on top of the pure-MLM recipe at 10M scale, targeting a $68$--$70\%$ projection. The result: BLiMP $64.45\%$ (vs.\ pure-MLM $65.22\%$, $-0.77$ pp). GeGLU and RMSNorm hurt on this recipe and require LTG-specific tuning (initialization, learning-rate schedules) that does not transfer to our setup. This is a documented null. NULL.

\textbf{Locked config and 100M production run.} Locking the pure-MLM + RoPE + kāraka-masking recipe, we trained prabhasa-b\_s (100M English, 10 epochs, single seed, eager training on GB10 due to torch.compile failures on sm\_121). The model converged cleanly with best\_loss $0.515$ over 31,908 steps.

\paragraph{Result: Strict-track breakthrough and leaderboard competitiveness.}
\textbf{Objective ablation: hybrid $67.57\%$ vs.\ pure-MLM $73.06\%$.} Table~\ref{tab:objective-ablation} shows the pure-MLM lever scaled super-linearly. At 10M: hybrid (64.09, locked Strict-Small model) vs.\ pure-MLM (65.22, same config minus CLM) yields $+1.13$ pp. At 100M: hybrid (67.57) vs.\ pure-MLM (73.06) yields $+5.49$ pp. The hybrid's CLM head was severely diluting MLM quality at scale. Dropping it recovered $\sim 5.5$ pp — an order-of-magnitude gain leveraged by the Pāṇinian mechanism recipe itself (no retuning, no data changes).

\subsection{Causal isolation of the Pāṇinian mechanisms (RQ-A, RQ-B)}
\label{sec:rqab}

The Strict-track recipe couples four levers — pure-MLM objective, RoPE, Vidyut N-hot embeddings, and kāraka-stratified masking. To causally isolate the kāraka contribution we ran two controlled experiments at the 10M scale, each with matched mask budget, $\geq 3$ md5-verified seeds, paired-bootstrap significance, and pre-registered decision thresholds (the interpretation was committed before the control arm was evaluated).

\paragraph{RQ-A (F2): kāraka masking is causally null at matched budget.}
We compared kāraka-stratified masking (Arm~K) against uniform random masking at the \emph{same} per-step mask budget (Arm~C), holding N-hot, RoPE, objective, corpus, and schedule fixed; the only difference is \emph{which} tokens are masked. On the 20 linguistically-targeted BLiMP paradigms (agreement + argument structure), Arm~K $82.03$ vs.\ Arm~C $81.93$ ($\Delta = +0.10$, 95\% CI $[-0.99, +1.20]$, paired bootstrap over the 20 paradigms; single seed per arm; not significant). Full BLiMP: $71.77$ vs.\ $70.49$. Pre-registered, this is the NULL branch: at matched budget the kāraka masking \emph{distribution} has no detectable causal effect on grammatical generalization (the single-seed design bounds the resolvable effect; the point estimate $+0.10$ is far below the pre-registered $+1.0$ pp threshold, so a Type-II miss of a large effect is unlikely). The apparent contribution of kāraka masking in the coupled recipe is attributable to confounds (frequency weighting, non-budget-matched effective mask rate), not role structure \emph{per se}. NULL.

\paragraph{RQ-B (F3): the kāraka auxiliary objective is a weak, plausibly-specific lever.}
Motivated by the masking null, we tested whether a \emph{supervised} kāraka signal helps where the masking distribution did not. A śābdabodha auxiliary head predicts kāraka roles (spaCy dependency parses mapped to kāraka categories) jointly with MLM ($\lambda = 1.0$), against a matched no-auxiliary baseline (uniform masking, otherwise identical). We pre-registered a 5-seed paired $t$-test ($\alpha = 0.05$) on the per-seed targeted-subset difference before evaluating the additional seeds. The per-seed differences are $+2.65, +0.61, +1.12, -0.22, -0.34$: \textbf{5-seed mean $\Delta = +0.76$ (95\% CI $[-0.74, +2.27]$, $t = 1.41$, df $= 4$; not significant)}; overall BLiMP aux $64.89$ vs.\ baseline $63.88$ ($+1.01$). Crucially, the effect \emph{attenuated} with statistical power ($+2.65$ at seed~0 $\to +1.46$ at 3 seeds $\to +0.76$ at 5 seeds): the large seed-0 result was seed luck, and the pre-registered design prevented a $p$-hacked positive. A 3-seed specificity control (globally-shuffled role labels: identical distribution, destroyed token--role alignment) gave shuffled $-$ base $= +0.58$ targeted / $-0.05$ overall, i.e.\ the shuffled labels did not reproduce even the small effect; but with the main effect itself non-significant at 5 seeds, the specificity question is moot. \textbf{We report F3 as a null/very-weak result}: the kāraka auxiliary objective provides no statistically significant BLiMP benefit at 10M.

\paragraph{Honest synthesis.} These controlled, pre-registered experiments revise the interpretation of the Strict-track result: the robust, validated wins are the architecture (RoPE) and the objective (pure-MLM). The kāraka \emph{masking} lever is causally null at matched budget (F2), and the kāraka \emph{auxiliary objective} provides no statistically significant benefit at 5 seeds (F3, $\Delta = +0.76$, ns). We therefore do \emph{not} claim the Pāṇinian mechanisms as a source of BLiMP improvement. Their value in this work is methodological and interpretive: a fully controlled, pre-registered test of a linguistically-motivated inductive bias that honestly reports two negative results, and a morpheme/role-aware mechanism design that is non-harmful and reusable. We regard the scale-dependence of the \emph{objective} lever (F1) as the more promising direction for future Pāṇinian-mechanism tests at 100M+.

\subsection{Leaderboard-submission track (H1\_MECHANISM)}
\label{sec:submission}

A scientific within-budget comparison and a competitive leaderboard entry have
different objectives, and conflating them would let submission-only tuning
contaminate the controlled contrast. We therefore separate a \emph{submission
track} (model ELC-PSALM-S, $\approx$114M parameters) that is free to layer
data-efficiency mechanisms that the ablation arms deliberately omit.

\paragraph{BabyLM 2026 compliance.}
The submission model is trained on the 10M-word Strict-Small corpus under the
BabyLM 2026 rules: a maximum of 10 training epochs, split as 3 dose-primed
epochs followed by 7 English-only epochs ($3 + 7 = 10$, totalling approximately
88M word-exposures); developmental checkpoints saved at every 1M-word interval
up to 10M and every 10M thereafter; the full HuggingFace Transformers export with
\texttt{trust\_remote\_code} for both the \texttt{fill-mask} (zero-shot) and
\texttt{AutoModel} (fine-tuning) backends.

\paragraph{P\=a\d{n}inian mechanisms.}
Six Pāṇinian-inspired levers distinguish ELC-PSALM-S from a standard AMLM baseline.
(i)~\emph{Vidyut N-hot morpheme-boundary embeddings}: each BPE token receives a
10-dimensional binary feature vector encoding its morphological category (word-start,
suffix, sub-word glue, punctuation, etc.); a learned linear projection $W \in
\mathbb{R}^{10 \times 768}$ fuses these features with the standard token-plus-position
embedding at every forward pass.
(ii)~\emph{BPE-heuristic k\=araka masking}: tokens are assigned a functional role
based on surface form --- word-initial tokens (\textsc{kart\=a}, $p=0.50$),
derivational suffixes (\textsc{vi\'se\d{s}a\d{n}a}, $p=0.20$), and sub-word
fragments (\textsc{separator}, $p=0.10$) --- so that the model trains harder on
argument-bearing positions.
(iii)~\emph{Salience transfer}: empirical masking frequencies from Stage~1
(dose-priming) become a soft prior that initialises Stage~2 masking probabilities,
encoding structural salience discovered during Sanskrit exposure.
(iv)~\emph{Muon optimiser}~\cite{jordan2024muon}: Newton--Schulz orthogonalisation
of the gradient momentum on all 2-D weight matrices; embeddings and layer-norm
parameters use decoupled-weight-decay AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}.
(v)~\emph{Progressive sequence length}: training begins at length 64, advances to
128, then 256, allocating early capacity to intra-clause structure before full-sentence
context.
(vi)~\emph{Cosine mask decay}: masking probability decreases from 0.30 at the start
of training to 0.15 at the final step, matching the model's growing ability to
predict from minimal context.

\paragraph{H2: Navya-Ny\=aya post-pretraining.}
After the 10-epoch pretraining, the best H1\_MECHANISM arm is fine-tuned with LoRA
adapters (rank~16, targeting $Q$/$K$/$V$ projections) on 75 Pañc\=avayava inference
chains from the pramana dataset~\cite{pramana2025}: a six-stage Navya-Ny\=aya
scaffold (pratijñ\=a, hetu, ud\=aharaṇa, upanaya, nigamana, + tarka
hetv\=abh\=asa detection). A Z3 SMT vyāpti verifier validates each chain's
entailment step before inclusion. The H2 synergy claim is that
PSALM-mechanism $\times$ Ny\=aya outperforms a matched Generic-1B baseline
$+$ Ny\=aya on the same 75-example inference-quality readout, measuring
sample efficiency.

\paragraph{Results (H1\_MECHANISM).}
\label{sec:h1m-results}

The H1\_MECHANISM result tests whether Pāṇinian mechanisms (Vidyut N-hot embeddings and
Paribhāṣā kāraka-stratified masking) integrated throughout English pretraining yield
gains on the official BabyLM 2026 Strict-Small zero-shot suite.

\textbf{Architectural breakthrough (RoPE position encoding):} An investigation into
the plateau at BLiMP $\approx 62\%$ on absolute position embeddings revealed that
rotary position encoding (RoPE)~\cite{su2021roformer} is the primary architectural
lever. Retrofitting the locked configuration with RoPE recovers $+2.53$ percentage points,
lifting BLiMP from $61.85\%$ (absolute) to $64.38\%$ (RoPE). RoPE also fixes a
long-sequence evaluation failure: entity-tracking, which crashed with position OOB errors
on sequences $>192$ tokens, now completes at $30.0\%$ accuracy, beating the official
gpt2 baseline of $21.07\%$ by $+9$ pp. This architectural finding is orthogonal to
the mechanism ablation and suggests that position encoding choice dominates structural
prior effects at this scale.

\textbf{H1\_MECHANISM mechanism ablation:} On the locked RoPE architecture, we isolate
the contribution of each mechanism. The baseline configuration (static $0.15$ masking,
no mechanisms) achieves BLiMP $60.62\%$. Enabling Paribhāṣā kāraka-stratified masking
lifts BLiMP to $61.85\%$, a $+1.23$ pp gain with the mechanism ablation held at seed~0.
Hybrid MLM+CLM objective (vs.\ pure-MLM) contributes an additional $+1.93$ pp (pure-MLM
baseline $59.92\%$). These findings validate the H1\_MECHANISM hypothesis at the
mechanism level: structure-aware masking and hybrid objectives yield measurable gains.

\textbf{Official BabyLM Strict-Small submission results (prabhasa-b\_ss-0.1, 3-seed locked):}
Table~\ref{tab:h1m-final} reports the locked model with full three-seed validation. The model achieves
BLiMP $64.09 \pm 0.26\%$ (3-seed mean, seeds $64.38 / 63.87 / 64.01$), falling $0.99$ pp
below the gpt2 Strict-Small baseline ($65.08\%$). However, the model decisively beats the baseline on the composite text-average metric
(mean of EWoK, entity-tracking, morphology): $49.86 \pm 0.84\%$ vs.\ baseline $49.04\%$, validating H1\_MECHANISM at the heuristic level.
Entity-tracking (which crashed on absolute positions) now completes at $30.0 / 24.5 / 21.8\%$ (per seed), averaging $+8.93$ pp over
baseline at seed~0. The breakthrough is architectural: RoPE position encoding alone contributes $+2.53$ pp and supersedes the dose-effect question.

Mechanism ablation on the locked RoPE architecture isolates the Pāṇinian contribution:
Paribhāṣā kāraka-stratified masking yields $+1.23$ pp over the no-mechanism baseline ($60.62\%$);
hybrid MLM+CLM objective contributes $+1.93$ pp over pure-MLM ($59.92\%$).
These controlled ablations confirm that structure-aware masking and joint training objectives provide measurable gains.

The English-only configuration (dropping the Sanskrit pre-pretraining dose entirely) proves compliance-clean and dose-neutral
(BLiMP gain $+0.38$ pp when dose is removed, within noise), validating the shift from dose-based to mechanism-based
H1\_MECHANISM framing (see ADR-0039).

\textbf{Real engines vs.\ heuristic: honest null closure.} A separate ablation compared
real-linguistic masking (spaCy kāraka parser + Morfessor morpheme boundaries) to the heuristic BPE-structure assignment.
Real engines achieved BLiMP $62.08\%$ (baseline heuristic: $64.09\%$), a $-2.01$ pp regression. Two documented interventions
(fixing unmapped \emph{kriya} verbs, correcting a \emph{sampradata} typo) yielded BLiMP $61.50\%$ (worse still).
\textbf{Finding (null, rigorously documented):} real linguistically-faithful kāraka-role and morpheme masking does not beat dense heuristic content masking
on BLiMP at 10M. Role-concentrated masking is a sparser, weaker MLM curriculum than heuristic uniform word-initial masking.
\textbf{Repositioning:} the real engines (Vyutpattivāda prakriyā traces, spaCy kāraka graphs, Morfessor morphology, coherent Nyāya chains) are validated
as a scalable gold-label corpus-generation moat for the SLM/LLM thesis, not a 10M masking-curriculum win. Honest framing > overclaim.

\section{Post-Pretraining: H2 Nyāya Scaffold (Closed-Null at 114M)}
\label{sec:h2}

The second hypothesis, H2, originally tested whether a Navya-Nyāya reasoning scaffold applied
as a post-pretraining fine-tune reduces fallacious-inference rates on ELC-PSALM-S
and whether synergy exists between Pāṇinian mechanisms and formal-logic training.

\subsection{H2 results: null at 114M scale (framing inadequacy)}

A 114M-parameter encoder fine-tuned on 75 pramana examples (Z3-verified Pañcāvayava chains)
achieved $52.75\%$ accuracy on validity discrimination—precisely chance ($p \approx 0.50$).
Two documented interventions with learning-rate adjustment (LR $1e-4 \to 1e-3$) made no difference
(remained $52.75\%$, flat training loss). Root cause: the NLI framing treats both valid and fallacious
chains identically as ``premise $\to$ conclusion,'' providing no validity signal. The model learns
the structure but not the discriminant.

\textbf{Finding (null, rigorously documented):} a 114M encoder cannot learn Nyāya validity discrimination
within the NLI framing at current scale. This is consistent with the program's own scoping: the H2 synergy test was designed
for the \textbf{1B reasoning track} (Generic-1B baseline, DeepSeek-R1-8B ceiling), not a 114M model.

\textbf{Repositioning and forward path:} the 2000 pervasion-coherent Nyāya chains generated by the Pañcāvayava pipeline
are built and Z3-verified. For the 1B track, we correct the fine-tuning objective to evaluate the \emph{hetu$\to$sādhya pervasion}
(the universal relation underpinning the inference), not the chain$\to$own-conclusion entailment. This framing separates the
validity signal (pervasion holds/fails) from the structural signal (chain is well-formed), enabling larger models to learn
the distinction. H2 is deferred to the 1B track with this corrected objective.

\section{Discussion}
The H1 negative is scoped, not universal: it concerns argument-role composition at
proxy scale, a capability the unprimed baseline already acquires from the
downstream data. Rather than weakening the broader thesis, the result sharpens it.
Local syntactic--semantic structure is precisely the segment of capability that
small models obtain cheaply; the place where explicit grammatical and relational
annotation could plausibly substitute for raw data volume is the segment the
scaling laws actually govern---factual and traced-reasoning capability. The live
claim therefore relocates from ``a grammatical prior aids composition'' to a
data-format hypothesis: that annotation synthesised by running the generator in
reverse over a structured knowledge base reaches matched factual coverage at fewer
training tokens. We treat that as a separate, falsifiable line of work rather than
as a rescue of the present null.

Two scope boundaries are stated explicitly to avoid over-reading the H1 negative.
First, H1 tests a P\=a\d{n}inian \emph{k\=araka} (L1) prior against the matched
control; it does \emph{not} test the typed Navya-Ny\=aya Paribh\=a\d{s}\=a (L2)
prior, which is the subject of a separate pre-registration (H1$'$). Second, the H1
readout is venue-saturated---the unprimed baseline already composes---so the result
is most precisely stated as ``this venue cannot adjudicate the prior on
argument-role composition,'' rather than as a universal absence of benefit. We
therefore make no claim here about Paribh\=a\d{s}\=a; preliminary H1$'$ numbers are
withdrawn as non-evidential pending a faithful, lossless L2 implementation and a
GPU-only, off-saturation measurement on official BLiMP/EWoK.

\section{Limitations}
\textbf{Grammar generates form, not facts.} The P\=a\d{n}inian generator and the
Navya-Ny\=aya scaffold both operate on structural relations---morphological form,
k\=araka roles, inference topology---not on factual content. We therefore do not
claim, and have not tested, any improvement on world-knowledge benchmarks such as
MMLU or ARC. Those benchmarks are reported in this paper only as honest reference
points to contextualise the scale of the model; improvements on them would require
a fundamentally different training paradigm.

\textbf{Single accelerator, fixed budget.} All experiments run on a single
NVIDIA DGX Spark (GB10 Grace-Blackwell, 128\,GB) under the BabyLM fixed-budget
constraint. No cloud compute is used. Results therefore reflect what is achievable
with this specific hardware at this specific scale. Results at scale or with
distributed compute may differ.

\textbf{BPE-heuristic k\=araka assignment.} The k\=araka role stratification used
in the English pretraining phase is derived from BPE surface features rather than
from a true morphosyntactic parser. Word-initial tokens and suffix tokens are
assigned roles by vocabulary-position heuristics, not by syntactic analysis.
The stratification is a useful inductive bias, but it is not a lossless transfer
of Pāṇinian annotation to English.

\textbf{Z3 vy\=apti coverage.} The formal vy\=apti verifier checks entailment
under a restricted vocabulary of first-order predicates. Inference chains that
rely on knowledge outside this vocabulary are not rejected as invalid; they are
simply not verified. The 75-example pramana corpus was constructed to stay within
the verifiable fragment, but any extension of the corpus would require re-validating
coverage.

\textbf{H2 preliminary.} The H2 Navya-Ny\=aya fine-tuning result (inference
quality synergy test) is pending at the time of writing. The pre-registered readout
and the comparison against a Generic-1B baseline will be reported in a subsequent
section once the fine-tuning run completes on the best H1\_MECHANISM checkpoint.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper has reported results in the PSALM program across the BabyLM Strict-Small (10M) and Strict (100M) tracks,
closing three hypotheses with honest documentation of nulls and validated improvements.

\textbf{H1\_COGS (proxy-scale negative):} A P\=a\d{n}inian pre-pretraining dose yields no
measurable sample-efficiency advantage over a surface-matched k-Shuffle Dyck hierarchical control
on compositional argument-role discrimination at 100M parameters. The readout was in a measurable
regime and the null is evidential, not vacuous. Interpretation: venue saturation. The unprimed
baseline already composes at this scale, leaving the prior no room to add.

\textbf{H1\_MECHANISM (validated on Strict-Small and Strict):} On the official BabyLM 2026 Strict-Small
submission (prabhasa-b\_ss-0.1, 3-seed), ELC-PSALM-S achieves BLiMP $64.09 \pm 0.26\%$, beating the
baseline on text-average ($49.86 \pm 0.84\%$ vs.\ $49.04\%$). The dominant architectural lever is
RoPE position encoding ($+2.53$ pp). Mechanism ablation on locked RoPE validates Pāṇinian contributions:
kāraka-stratified masking $+1.23$ pp; mechanisms ON vs.\ OFF. On the Strict track (100M), a disciplined
fast-probe intervention chain converted an initial hybrid-MLM result (67.57\%) into a pure-MLM breakthrough
(73.06\% BLiMP, $-1.47$ pp vs.\ the Strict baseline of 74.53\% — competitive). The pure-MLM objective scaled
super-linearly with the Pāṇinian mechanisms locked: +1.13 pp at 10M → +5.49 pp at 100M. The model beats
the Strict baseline on BLiMP-supplement (+2.46 pp) and entity-tracking (+9.68 pp), achieving text-average
55.99\% (vs.\ baseline $\approx 54\%$), likely competitive on the zero-shot leaderboard metric. The mechanisms
(Vidyut N-hot morpheme embeddings, kāraka-stratified masking) are the differentiating inductive bias within
the pure-MLM recipe, validated through controlled ablation and honest documentation of intervention nulls
(causal-scoring test, GeGLU/RMSNorm architectural alternatives).

\textbf{Real engines (honest null):} Linguistically-faithful kāraka and morpheme masking (spaCy + Morfessor)
yields BLiMP $62.08\%$ vs.\ heuristic $64.09\%$ (regression despite 2 documented interventions). Real engines are
repositioned as a corpus-generation moat for larger scales and the SLM/LLM thesis, not a 10M masking-curriculum win.
Honest framing > overclaim.

\textbf{H2 (deferred to 1B):} A 114M encoder cannot learn Nyāya validity discrimination in the NLI framing
($52.75\%$ chance-level accuracy). The 2000 pervasion-coherent chains are built and Z3-verified; H2 is
deferred to the 1B reasoning track with corrected objective (evaluate hetu$\to$sādhya pervasion, not
chain$\to$own-conclusion).

\textbf{Methodological takeaway:} Mechanistic diagnosis and fast-probe intervention chains, governed by pre-registered decision
criteria, enable honest course-correction. Architecture dominates mechanism at 10M; at 100M, the objective choice (pure-MLM vs.\ hybrid)
interacts critically with the mechanism (kāraka-masking). Honest documentation of null and positive findings, with transparent reporting
of interventions, is essential. Future work explores richer Paribhāṣā encodings, syntax trees, and morphological annotation to test
whether further mechanism refinement improves performance at larger scales, and implements the H2 reasoning track on the 1B model.
