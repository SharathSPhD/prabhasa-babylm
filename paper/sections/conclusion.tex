\section{Conclusion}

Prabhasa is a controlled, pre-registered evaluation of whether Pañinian morphosyntactic structure, operationalized through morpheme-boundary embeddings and role-aware masking, improves data-efficient pretraining of small language models. The experiments isolate architectural effects (RoPE), objective effects (pure MLM), and mechanism effects (kāraka masking and auxiliary supervision) through matched-budget comparisons and multi-seed validation.

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

Three empirical findings emerge from the complete experimental run.

\emph{F1: The objective effect is scale-dependent.} Removing the causal language modeling (CLM) head in favor of pure masked-language modeling (MLM) yields no significant difference at 10M parameters (pure-MLM 63.58 \ensuremath{\pm} 1.73 vs.\ hybrid 64.09 \ensuremath{\pm} 0.26, overlapping 95\% confidence intervals on the BLiMP suite) but a substantial gain at 100M parameters (73.06 vs.\ 67.57, a 5.49 percentage point difference). This finding motivates the use of pure-MLM training for the Strict-track 100M model while retaining the hybrid objective for the Strict-Small 10M submission. The result suggests that the dilutive effect of the CLM head on MLM gradient flow becomes material at larger scales, consistent with multitask learning literature \cite{Kaplan2020}.

\emph{F2: Kāraka-aware masking is causally null at matched budget.} A pre-registered controlled comparison between role-stratified masking (Arm K) and uniform random masking (Arm C), with all other factors held constant (budget, architecture, objective, corpus), yields a negligible difference on the targeted agreement and argument-structure paradigms (82.03 vs.\ 81.93, 95\% CI [\(-0.99, +1.20\)]) and full BLiMP (71.77 vs.\ 70.49). The apparent gains claimed earlier in development were confounded by mask-rate variation and frequency weighting, not role structure itself. This null result is a well-controlled closure of a pre-registered hypothesis.

\emph{F3: Kāraka auxiliary objectives show weak and non-significant effects.} A pre-registered 5-seed paired $t$-test on the supervised kāraka-role prediction auxiliary objective yields a mean difference of +0.76 percentage points on targeted paradigms (95\% CI [\(-0.74, +2.27\)], $t = 1.41$, $\text{df} = 4$, not significant). The effect attenuated with power (+2.65 for seed 0, +0.76 across 5 seeds), indicating that the initial single-seed result was seed-driven variance rather than a robust signal. A shuffled-role control confirmed that the small effect was not generic multitask regularization. Overall BLiMP on the auxiliary objective reached 64.89 vs.\ baseline 63.88 (+1.01), within the noise margin of development.

The primary conclusion is that architecture (RoPE) and objective design (pure MLM at scale) are the primary levers for improving BabyLM performance at the 10M-100M parameter regime, while the Pañinian mechanisms (morpheme-aware masking and supervised role prediction) do not carry measurable causal weight on standard linguistic diagnostics. This is a valid scientific outcome from pre-registered, multi-seed, well-controlled experiments.

\subsection{Future Work}

Four concrete research directions remain open.

\emph{Scale and structure interaction at 100M.} The scale-dependent objective effect (F1) motivates a full replication of the kāraka auxiliary objective experiment at 100M parameters with extended pretraining (50M+ tokens per seed). At larger scales and with longer training, the model may develop greater capacity to exploit role-structure signals. This experiment is essential for determining whether the non-significance of F3 at 10M is a scale artifact or a fundamental property of the auxiliary objective.

\emph{Syntax-specific evaluation.} Evaluation on agreement and argument-structure diagnostics (BLiMP subsets) may not be the optimal readout for structure-aware pretraining. Compositional generalization benchmarks (SCAN, COGS, CFQ) are better suited to reveal effects of morphosyntactic structure. A targeted replication comparing Pañinian mechanisms to baselines on these benchmarks would test whether the null BLiMP finding generalizes or whether the mechanism provides benefits on other evaluation surfaces.

\emph{Multilingual BabyLM track.} Prabhasa is evaluated on English-only pretraining. The linguistic design of Pañinian kāraka roles suggests that effects may be larger in morphologically rich languages (Sanskrit, Finnish, Turkish) or in zero-shot cross-lingual transfer settings. A multilingual variant using Sanskrit pre-pretraining (structured role annotations from the Sanskrit Heritage Lexicon \cite{Huet2003SanskritHeritage}) followed by English fine-tuning would test whether structure-aware mechanisms show stronger signatures when applied to syntactically complex source languages.

\emph{Navya-Nyāya reasoning scaffold and inference-quality readout (H2).} The original PSALM program includes a Navya-Nyāya logical reasoning scaffold designed to improve inferential validity via a 6-phase Pañcāvayava fine-tuning pipeline. This component is out of scope for BabyLM but represents a distinct evaluation surface where Pañinian structure may provide benefits beyond standard supervised benchmarks. A targeted H2 experiment on fallacy detection and inference-quality metrics (tested on a small annotated corpus of logical-validity judgments or on the subset of BLiMP that probes inferential consistency) would measure whether the best-performing H1 arm (pure-MLM at 100M) synergizes with the H2 scaffold.

\subsection{Limitations}

The primary limitation is the small parameter range (10M to 100M). While the BabyLM budget is by design constrained, effects that emerge only at larger scales (beyond 350M parameters) would not be detected. The scale-dependent objective effect (F1) shows that large parameter differences can change mechanism efficacy; the current study is therefore underpowered to rule out structure-aware mechanisms at frontier scales.

A second limitation is the restricted evaluation scope. BLiMP focuses on agreement and argument structure; compositional generalization, semantic role labeling, and cross-lingual transfer are not measured. The null findings on BLiMP do not rule out effects on these other surfaces.

A third limitation is the single-seed nature of the Strict-track submission model (100M pure-MLM, BLiMP 73.06). While the objective-effect comparison (F1) uses 3 seeds and the kāraka effects (F2, F3) use either 1 seed per arm or 5 seeds, the final submitted model represents a single instantiation of the best arm. Replication with multiple seeds would strengthen the Strict-track baseline claims.
