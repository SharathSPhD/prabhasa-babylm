Prabhāsa builds on research into data-efficient pretraining, morphologically-informed linguistic structure, and formal grammatical frameworks in neural language modelling. Three research areas establish the context: the BabyLM controlled pretraining paradigm, which isolates training methodology from data scale through a fixed 10M-token budget and evaluation on compositional tasks (SCAN, COGS, CFQ, BLiMP) and standard NLP benchmarks (GLUE); morphology-aware masking strategies, wherein masking probability conditions on linguistic structure (morpheme boundaries, syntactic depth, or semantic roles) rather than uniform randomness, as explored by Søgaard et al.\ and recent BabyLM submissions (LTG-BERT, ELC-BERT, GPT-BERT) with gains on structured tasks; and the Pāṇinian framework, a formal grammar from circa 500 BCE defining Sanskrit word formation, phonological changes, and argument structure (kāraka roles), computationally implemented via the Vidyut toolkit. The official BabyLM baselines (Strict 74.53 BLiMP, Strict-Small 65.08 BLiMP)~\cite{Hu2024BabyLM} establish reference points for competitive submissions.

A growing body of work questions whether structural priors, including rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) for length generalization, causal versus masked language modelling objectives, and morpheme-aware tokenisation schemes, can improve sample efficiency at fixed compute budgets. Morphologically rich languages like Sanskrit present natural testbeds: the kāraka system defines semantic roles purely by relationship to the predicate, making it a target for role-aware pretraining; the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit and GRETIL collection provide large annotated corpora; and Huet's Sanskrit Heritage platform enables measurement of morphological competence (sandhi, agreement, stem-inflection pairing). Classical Indian logic (Navya-Nyāya), a formal system of semantics and reference developed from the 13th century, provides a post-training reasoning scaffold (Pañcāvayava fine-tuning) for improving compositional inference, as discussed by Matilal and Ganeri, and represents a novel post-training axis orthogonal to contemporary fine-tuning templates. For completeness, see Appendix~\ref{app:repro} for the full survey of morpheme-aware tokenisation (Botha and Blunsom, Yaltik et al.), optimiser design (Muon), causal-objective research (Clemons et al.), and Sanskrit computational linguistics (Goyal and Huet, Kulkarni et al.).

The novelty lies in integration rather than isolated components. Prior work on data-efficient pretraining and structural priors has been confined to well-resourced languages; morpheme-aware masking has been explored without explicit coupling to semantic role systems or classical grammatical frameworks. Prabhāsa synthesises three elements: Pāṇinian morphological tokenisation and kāraka-aware adaptive masking during English pretraining, evaluated on the BabyLM regime against the official leaderboard suite; evaluation of RoPE and objective choice (pure-MLM versus hybrid) as architectural levers; and post-training Navya-Nyāya reasoning scaffolds, grounded in classical Indian logic rather than modern templates. Each component has precedent; their integration and measurement under pre-registered hypothesis tests at 10M and 100M scales is the contribution.
