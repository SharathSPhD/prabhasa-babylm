# Sanskrit Vyākaraṇa as a Generative Data Engine for Sample-Efficient Foundation Models: A Research Landscape and DGX Spark Prototype Plan

## TL;DR
- **The core hypothesis is partially supported in spirit but unproven at the target scale, and its load-bearing claim — that grammar-derived data can beat internet-scale data to reach Llama-3/Gemma/Claude-Haiku-class *general* reasoning on a single DGX Spark — is almost certainly false; the realistic, publishable win is *sample efficiency and compositional generalization*, not frontier parity.** The closest validated precedent (Hu, Petty, Shi, Merrill & Linzen, "Between Circuits and Chomsky," ACL 2025) shows formal-language *pre-pretraining* cuts the natural-language token budget by **33% for a 1B model trained on ~1.6B tokens** — a real but modest lever, not an order-of-magnitude one.
- **The specific novel contribution — using Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī as an unbounded generative engine emitting grammatically-guaranteed sentences with built-in kāraka/parse-tree annotations as LM (pre-)pretraining data — has not been done.** The generator that makes it possible (Kulkarni & Pai's Sanskrit Sentence Generator, 2019, in the Saṃsādhanī platform) exists and already emits dependency/kāraka parses, but no one has wired it to neural-LM pretraining. That gap is the project's defensible originality.
- **On a 128GB GB10 DGX Spark, the feasible, honest program is a 100M–1B-parameter model in a BabyLM-style controlled-data regime**, testing whether Paninian synthetic data plus a small natural-language corpus beats a *matched* natural-language-only baseline (and a Dyck-language control) on BLiMP, GLUE, EWoK, and compositional benchmarks (SCAN/COGS/CFQ) — not on MMLU parity with Haiku.

## Key Findings

1. **Small-data pretraining works, within sharply bounded limits.** BabyLM's 2024 winner GPT-BERT (Charpentier & Samuel, "BERT or GPT: why not both?", CoNLL BabyLM 2024, arXiv:2410.24159) won *both* tracks, reaching composite scores of **81.2 on the 10M-word "Strict-Small" track and 86.1 on the 100M-word "Strict" track** (a hybrid masked+causal objective with a 15:1 causal:MLM example ratio), outperforming models trained on trillions of words — but on grammatical/understanding benchmarks (BLiMP, GLUE, EWoK), *not* on world-knowledge benchmarks like MMLU. Phi-1 (1.3B params, ~7B unique tokens, ~50B tokens seen, 4 days on 8×A100) hit 50.6% HumanEval. Capability-per-token can be pushed hard with high-signal data — but only for *form/skill*, not broad *knowledge*.

2. **Formal-grammar pre-pretraining is a real, validated lever — but a ~1.3–1.5× one, not 10×.** Hu et al. (ACL 2025, arXiv:2502.19249) found that pre-pretraining a 1B model on *k*-Shuffle Dyck (a context-sensitive language definable in C-RASP, which length-generalizes) "achieves the same loss and better linguistic generalization with a 33% smaller token budget" versus a baseline trained on ~1.6B natural-language tokens. Transfer requires the formal language to (a) capture hierarchical dependencies and (b) be learnable by the transformer architecture. Earlier work (Papadimitriou & Jurafsky 2020/2023; Chiang & Lee 2022; Ri & Tsuruoka, ACL 2022) corroborates that nesting/dependency structure is what transfers, and that context-sensitive languages transfer best.

3. **Sanskrit grammar as a generative engine is feasible and unexplored for LM training.** Kulkarni & Pai's Sanskrit Sentence Generator (2019, 6th ISCLS, ACL Anthology W19-7501) already produces grammatical sentences *with* dependency/kāraka parses from a meaning structure using Paninian rules; Pai's UoH PhD (2020) implements specific sūtras for kāraka→case assignment; the Saṃsādhanī/SCL toolkit (Amba Kulkarni, UoH) and Huet's INRIA Heritage Platform implement morphology generation, sandhi, and kāraka analysis. But **every neural Sanskrit model to date trains *on existing texts*, never on grammar-generated synthetic data**: SansGPT (Chaudhari et al., ICON 2024) is a ~75M-param GPT pretrained on ~51M real tokens from DCS+GRETIL; ByT5-Sanskrit (Nehrdich, Hellwig et al., arXiv:2409.13920) fine-tunes on DCS annotations. No prior work uses Paninian generation as a pretraining data engine.

4. **The total machine-readable Sanskrit corpus is tiny by LLM standards — which is precisely the argument for a generative grammar.** The Digital Corpus of Sanskrit holds "about 5,660,000 manually tagged words in 755,000 text lines" with full morphological and lexical analysis; GRETIL adds roughly low-tens-of-millions of mostly-unannotated words (no official count); a defensible grand total of digitized Sanskrit is ~30–50M words, against ~30M extant un-digitized manuscripts. "Train on Sanskrit text" therefore cannot anchor a foundation model — but a *generative* grammar emitting unbounded, parse-annotated text sidesteps the corpus-size ceiling.

5. **DGX Spark reality check.** The DGX Spark uses the GB10 Grace Blackwell Superchip (20 Arm cores: 10×Cortex-X925 + 10×A725), ~1 PFLOP FP4 (sparse), and **128GB unified LPDDR5x across 16 channels (256-bit) at LPDDR5X-8533, delivering 273 GB/s** — ~31 TFLOPS FP32, GB10 SoC TDP 140W, 4TB NVMe, ~$3,999. NVIDIA's User Guide positions it for fine-tuning (up to ~70B) and inference on models up to ~200B — **not** from-scratch frontier pretraining. It can comfortably pretrain 100M–1B models from scratch over days-to-weeks; a Chinchilla-optimal 1B needs ~20B tokens.

## Details

### Thread 1 — Minimal corpus / small-data pretraining
- **BabyLM (2023, 2024):** Fixed budgets of 10M ("Strict-Small") and 100M ("Strict") words, inspired by human acquisition. 2023 winner: ELC-BERT (an LTG-BERT variant; Charpentier & Samuel 2023). 2024 winner of both tracks: GPT-BERT (composite 81.2 / 86.1). BabyLlama-2 (345M, ensemble-distilled on 9.5M words) outperformed its teachers and the 10M/100M baselines. Lesson: architecture + training objective + distillation, not scale, drive this regime — and benchmarks are grammar/understanding (BLiMP, GLUE, EWoK), *not* MMLU world knowledge.
- **Phi family:** Phi-1 ("Textbooks Are All You Need," Gunasekar et al., arXiv:2306.11644) — 1.3B params, 6B filtered web "textbook-quality" tokens + 1B GPT-3.5-synthetic tokens, ~8 passes (~50B tokens seen), 50.6% HumanEval / 55.5% MBPP; phi-1-base (no fine-tune) already 29% HumanEval. Phi-3-mini (3.8B, **3.3T tokens**, 69% MMLU); phi-3-small/medium (7B/14B, **4.8T tokens**, 75%/78% MMLU). **Critical caveat:** Phi's *general* capability still required trillions of tokens — the "textbook" approach compresses *web* data; it does not escape scale for world knowledge.
- **Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., NeurIPS 2022):** ~20 tokens/parameter compute-optimal (>400 models, 70M–16B; replicated by Epoch AI 2024 and Cerebras-GPT 111M–13B). A 1B compute-optimal model ⇒ ~20B tokens. 2024 Mosaic/Tsinghua analyses argue for *higher* ratios with quality data; "Reconciling Kaplan and Chinchilla" (Pearce & Song, arXiv:2406.12907) reaffirms the ~20:1 coefficient. Implication: a conventional from-scratch "competitive" 1B wants ≥20B tokens — the hypothesis must beat this.
- **Densing Law (Xiao et al., Nature Machine Intelligence vol. 7, issue 11, Nov 2025; arXiv:2412.04315):** "capability density" (effective params / actual params) for open-source base LLMs "doubles approximately every 3.5 months" (fit across 51 base LLMs released Feb 2023–Apr 2025, R²≈0.93), driven primarily by data scale/quality. Supports "quality over quantity" but frames it as *incremental, compounding* efficiency — not a single-shot 100× data cut.

### Thread 2 — Formal grammar as structural prior / generative source
- **Strongest precedent — Hu et al. (ACL 2025):** pre-pretraining on context-sensitive *k*-Shuffle Dyck (in C-RASP, length-generalizing) → 33% token savings + better syntactic generalization for a 1B model. Defines the theoretical sweet spot: the intersection of the Chomsky hierarchy (expressive enough for hierarchical dependencies) and the circuit-complexity hierarchy (learnable by transformers; C-RASP ⊂ FO(M)).
- **Procedural/synthetic pretraining:** Neural Cellular Automata pre-pre-training (arXiv:2603.10055) improves downstream LM by up to 6% and converges up to 1.6× faster on only 164M NCA tokens — surprisingly *outperforming* 1.6B Common Crawl tokens. "Procedural Pretraining: Warming Up LMs with Abstract Data" (arXiv:2601.21725) generalizes the warm-up framing.
- **Compositional generalization:** Lake & Baroni (Nature, 2023, "Human-like systematic generalization through a meta-learning neural network") — Meta-Learning for Compositionality (MLC): a standard transformer trained on a *stream of grammars* (a meta-grammar) achieves human-like systematic generalization. Benchmarks: SCAN, COGS/ReCOGS, CFQ. Kim et al. (2022) warn that pretrained models over-state compositional ability via lexical exposure, so training-from-scratch evaluation is methodologically cleaner.
- **Code/math transfer:** structured domains (code, Java, MIDI) transfer to NL (Papadimitriou & Jurafsky 2020); abstract syntactic similarity is the mechanism.

### Thread 3 — Pāṇini as a generative computational system
- **Computational power:** The Aṣṭādhyāyī (~3,959 sūtras across 8 chapters) is a generative, rule-complete system with metarules (paribhāṣā), transformations, and recursion — informally likened to a Turing machine and to BNF/production grammars (Britannica; Cardona, Staal). Cardona (1965/1969) and Staal (1965) formalized its context-sensitive metarule structure. Best treated as (at least) context-sensitive — exactly the Chomsky-hierarchy class Hu et al. found transfers best.
- **Generation tooling that exists:** Kulkarni & Pai Sanskrit Sentence Generator (2019; meaning-structure → grammatical sentence + dependency/kāraka parse); Pai PhD (2020, UoH); Saṃsādhanī/SCL toolkit (morphology generator, sandhi joiner/splitter, kāraka analyzer; github.com/samsaadhanii/scl); Heritage Platform (Huet, INRIA — 130k flexed forms from 12k stems in 2001; later a 700k-form lexicon). PAIAS and Sohoni–Kulkarni Haskell simulators implement derivation/word-formation.
- **Corpus sizes:** DCS ~5.66M manually tagged words / 755k lines; GRETIL low-tens-of-millions, mostly unannotated, no official count; a Buddhist+general reference corpus reports ~13M (general) + ~7M (Buddhist) words; SARIT is small (dozens of TEI texts). Grand total digitized ≈ 30–50M words.
- **Key gap:** Nobody has used Paninian generation as an *unbounded* training-data engine for neural LMs. This is the project's novelty.

### Thread 4 — Curriculum learning & structural priors
- Bengio et al. (2009) curriculum learning; easy-to-hard ordering helps in structured regimes. Hu et al.'s pre-pretraining is itself a 2-stage curriculum (formal → natural). BabyLM motivates developmentally-plausible ordering. Open question the project can own: does *grammar-first* curriculum build a better reasoning "backbone" than mixed data?

### Thread 5 — Tokenization & representation efficiency
- MorphBPE (arXiv:2502.00894, 2025): morphology-aware BPE reduces cross-entropy and accelerates convergence at 300M & 1B across English/Russian/Hungarian/Arabic. "Fertility" (tokens/word) strongly predicts accuracy; each unit increase can cost 8–18 points (Lundin et al. 2025). "Is Sanskrit the Most Token-Efficient Language?" (arXiv:2601.06142, 2026) finds production tokenizers (cl100k_base, o200k_base, Gemini) *fail* to capture Sanskrit's compactness, while SentencePiece trained on Sanskrit does. Implication: a sandhi/morpheme-aware Sanskrit tokenizer is a necessary co-design and itself a contribution.

### Thread 6 — Neurosymbolic / grammar-constrained training
- Grammar-constrained decoding (GCD), CFG-guided generation, and parse-tree auxiliary losses are mature. The Paninian generator supplies *free gold parse trees and kāraka roles*, enabling parse-prediction auxiliary heads and grammatical-correctness verification as a training signal — a natural neurosymbolic fit.

### Thread 7 — Competing / parallel hypotheses
- **LIMA (Zhou et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2305.11206):** 1,000 examples (~750k tokens) fine-tune LLaMA-65B to equal/preferred-over-DaVinci003 outputs (43% vs GPT-4, 58% vs Bard). But this *surfaces pretrained knowledge via alignment* — it is not from-scratch pretraining. Reinforces: knowledge comes from pretraining; format/style is cheap.
- **Grokking (Power et al. 2022, arXiv:2201.02177):** delayed generalization on small algorithmic datasets, well past overfitting — suggests structured small corpora *can* generalize with extended optimization; Omnigrok (arXiv:2210.01117) shows the dramatic effect largely vanishes on realistic noisy data.
- **Counter-evidence (decisive):** Phi needs trillions of tokens for MMLU; world knowledge and broad semantics cannot be derived from grammar — grammar generates *form*, not *facts*. A grammar engine produces syntactically valid but semantically/world-empty sentences. This is the central reason frontier-parity on *knowledge* benchmarks is implausible.

### Thread 8 — DGX Spark implementation
- **Hardware:** GB10 Grace Blackwell, 20 Arm cores, 128GB unified LPDDR5X-8533 over 16 channels @ 273 GB/s, ~1 PFLOP FP4 sparse, ~31 TFLOPS FP32, 4TB NVMe, GB10 TDP 140W (240W PSU), ~$3,999. Two units link via ConnectX-7 200GbE (256GB combined).
- **Bottleneck:** memory bandwidth (273 GB/s) — roughly an order of magnitude below an H100 (~3 TB/s). Training throughput is modest; this is a prototyping box, not a pretraining cluster.
- **Frameworks:** NeMo, Megatron-LM, TorchTitan, LitGPT/Fabric, Nanotron; BF16 mixed precision, Flash Attention, gradient checkpointing, and μP/μTransfer to port hyperparameters from cheap small proxies.
- **Feasible scope:** from-scratch 100M–1B models; 1B compute-optimal ≈ 20B tokens; expect days-to-weeks per run given bandwidth limits. 7B from-scratch is impractical on one unit.

### Thread 9 — Heterodox / information-theoretic framings
- "One epoch is enough" with sufficiently diverse data; MDL framing — Pāṇini as a near-minimal-description-length encoding of Sanskrit. The information-theoretic pitch: a finite ~4k-sūtra grammar is a compressed generative prior. Whether its inductive bias transfers to *English/general* reasoning is the empirical question Hu et al. partially answered (yes for syntax; unknown for semantics/reasoning).

## The Central Enabling Insight
The hypothesis's distinctive move — **grammar as data engine, not Sanskrit as domain** — is genuinely novel and technically buildable. The nearest analogues are (1) Hu et al.'s formal-language pre-pretraining (validated, +33% token efficiency, but on artificial Dyck languages with no semantics) and (2) Lake & Baroni's meta-grammar training (validated for compositional generalization). Pāṇini's grammar is strictly richer than Dyck: it encodes morphology, agreement, free word order resolved by kāraka roles, and recursion — i.e., it natively contains the hierarchical and cross-serial dependencies that theory says transfer best, *and* it comes with free gold parse/kāraka annotations. No one has combined these. **The barriers: (a) semantics/world-knowledge cannot come from grammar (this caps general-benchmark ceilings); (b) the generation diversity/coverage of the Saṃsādhanī generator is unproven at corpus scale; (c) the realized transfer gain may be the modest ~1.3× of Hu et al. rather than the order-of-magnitude the hypothesis asserts.**

## Critical Counter-Evidence
1. **World knowledge is not grammatical.** MMLU/ARC-Challenge demand facts; Phi shows these need trillions of web tokens. Grammar-derived data is semantically hollow.
2. **Observed transfer gains are modest** (33% token savings; ~6% perplexity for NCA), not 10–100×.
3. **Pretrained-model compositional gains are routinely overestimated** (Kim et al. 2022) — evaluate from scratch.
4. **Sanskrit's real corpus is tiny** (~5.66M annotated words) and cannot anchor semantics; synthetic generation risks distributional narrowness and grammar-engine artifacts (template repetition, missing pragmatics).
5. **Gemma-/Claude-Haiku-class parity is a category error** for a 1B-from-scratch model on a 128GB / 273 GB/s box.

## Prototype Research Plan for DGX Spark
**Reframed, defensible claim:** "Paninian-generated, parse-annotated synthetic data is a more token-efficient structural prior than matched natural-language or matched formal-language (Dyck) data for sample-efficient LMs, improving compositional and syntactic generalization." Target *efficiency and generalization*, not frontier parity.

- **Stage 0 — Tooling (weeks 1–4):** Wrap the Kulkarni–Pai / Saṃsādhanī generator into a streaming corpus generator emitting (sentence, kāraka parse, derivation trace). Build a sandhi/morpheme-aware SentencePiece tokenizer (~16–32k vocab). Quantify generation diversity/coverage (type-token ratio, construction coverage) before committing compute.
- **Stage 1 — Controlled proxy (weeks 5–10):** Train 100M-param decoders from scratch (μP-tuned) under a BabyLM-style fixed budget. Arms: **(A)** NL-only baseline (10M/100M words); **(B)** Paninian-synthetic pre-pretraining → NL; **(C)** Dyck / *k*-Shuffle Dyck pre-pretraining → NL (direct replication of Hu et al. as the key control); **(D)** Paninian + parse-tree auxiliary loss. Eval: BLiMP, BLiMP-supplement, GLUE, EWoK, SCAN, COGS/ReCOGS, CFQ. Primary metrics: tokens-to-target-loss and compositional accuracy vs. baseline.
- **Stage 2 — Scale to 1B (weeks 11–20):** Promote the best arm to 1B params, ~20B-token-equivalent. Report the token-efficiency curve and whether the Paninian prior *beats Dyck*. Report ARC-Easy, HellaSwag, and MMLU honestly as absolute reference points — expect MMLU near chance (~25–35%); that is the expected, defensible outcome for a grammar-anchored model.
- **Go/no-go thresholds:** Proceed to Stage 2 only if Stage 1 shows **≥15% token savings over the Dyck control** (i.e., Sanskrit's added morphology/kāraka structure beats plain hierarchical brackets) **or a ≥3-point compositional-generalization gain** over baseline. If Paninian ≈ Dyck, the scientific contribution narrows to "Sanskrit behaves like a rich Dyck language" — still publishable, but reframe accordingly.

## Publication Strategy
- **Strongest framing:** "A morphologically-rich, parse-annotated *natural*-language grammar as a structural prior for sample-efficient pretraining" — positioned directly against Hu et al. (ACL 2025) and Lake & Baroni (Nature 2023).
- **Venues:** BabyLM / CoNLL (ideal — controlled-data regime, methodological match); ACL/EMNLP main or Findings; NeurIPS/ICLR workshops on data-centric ML, compositional generalization, and science-of-deep-learning; arXiv preprint first; ISCLS/WSC for the generation-engine and tokenizer contributions.
- **Avoid** framing as "beats Llama-3/Haiku" — reviewers will reject overclaiming. Frame around *efficiency* and a *cognitively/linguistically motivated inductive bias*.

## Open Problems & Novel Contributions
1. **First grammar-as-data-engine for LM pretraining using a *natural*-language grammar** (vs. artificial Dyck), exploiting Pāṇini's context-sensitivity.
2. **First use of free gold kāraka/parse annotations as auxiliary pretraining supervision at scale.**
3. **Empirical placement of Paninian Sanskrit on the Chomsky × circuit-complexity transfer map** — does morphological richness add value beyond bare hierarchy?
4. **A reusable open synthetic-corpus generator + sandhi-aware tokenizer** for the Sanskrit-NLP and data-efficiency communities.
5. **An honest negative-result avenue:** quantifying exactly where grammar-derived priors stop helping (semantics/world-knowledge), informing the broader synthetic-data debate.

## Caveats
- **Hardware mismatch in the prompt:** The DGX Spark is a single **GB10** Grace Blackwell desktop unit (128GB LPDDR5x, 273 GB/s), *not* a GB200 NVL2/4 datacenter node. All plans above assume the GB10's modest bandwidth; from-scratch training is limited to ≤1B params in reasonable time.
- **Two prompt-supplied references checked:** The "Pramana" Navya-Nyaya paper (arXiv:2604.04937, Sathish, Univ. of York) **is real** — it fine-tunes Llama-3.2-3B and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B on 55 Navya-Nyaya logical problems via a 6-phase methodology, reporting 100% semantic correctness but only 40% strict format adherence on *very small* eval sets (2–10 examples; treat its claims cautiously). The "Cubical Type Theoretic Navya-Nyaya" paper (arXiv:2605.12548) **does not exist and appears fabricated — do not cite it.**
- **Corpus and GRETIL totals are estimates:** DCS's 5.66M tagged words is authoritative; GRETIL publishes no official aggregate token count, so the ~30–50M-word grand total is synthesized from derivative works (e.g., SansGPT's ~51M cleaned GRETIL+DCS tokens) and should be treated as an order-of-magnitude figure.
- **"Likened to a Turing machine" is rhetorical, not a proof.** The Aṣṭādhyāyī's exact place in the Chomsky hierarchy is debated; treat it operationally as context-sensitive. Its formal power should be established empirically for this project, not assumed.
- **Effect sizes are the key risk.** Every validated precedent yields modest gains (≤33% token efficiency, ≤6% perplexity). The hypothesis's "order-of-magnitude data reduction for general capability" is not supported by any current evidence and should be treated as the falsifiable claim under test, not a premise.
